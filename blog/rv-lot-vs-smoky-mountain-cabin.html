RV Lot vs. Smoky Mountain Cabin: Which Investment Actually Cash Flows?

Both sit in one of the most visited tourism corridors in the country. But the numbers underneath are very different. Here's an honest side-by-side.

The Smoky Mountains are a proven short-term rental market. Gatlinburg and Pigeon Forge draw tens of millions of visitors annually, and both cabin rentals and RV resort lots have produced real investor returns here. But "the Smokies work" is not the same as "these two asset classes work the same way." The entry cost, debt structure, operating complexity, and breakeven occupancy are different enough that buyers should model them separately before choosing.

Purchase price and entry costs

Smoky Mountain cabins — particularly one- to three-bedroom units with mountain views or hot tubs — typically list between $350,000 and $900,000. Entry-level cabins in high-demand areas start around $400K and have trended up significantly over the past five years. You will also pay closing costs, inspection fees, and likely furnishing and setup costs if buying a non-turnkey property.

RV lots at Outdoor Resorts Gatlinburg are a meaningfully different price point. Lot pricing varies by position — creekside commands the highest premiums, interior lots offer lower entry — but the overall range is substantially below cabin pricing. Buyers placing an existing camper they already own reduce their entry cost further, with only the lot acquisition to finance.

Debt service comparison

Cabin financing typically uses a conventional mortgage or vacation home loan. At $500K purchase, 20% down, a 30-year mortgage at 7.5% produces monthly principal and interest of roughly $2,800. Annual debt service: approximately $33,600.

An ORG lot financed via a land loan or HELOC at a lower purchase price produces meaningfully lower monthly obligations. If you're placing an existing camper, you carry only lot debt. This structural difference is the core of the cash-flow comparison.

Cabins command higher nightly rates, but they also carry higher per-night operating costs: more square footage to clean, more surfaces to maintain, higher utility bills, and more complex guest turnover. RV lots have lower gross revenue per night but also simpler operations — cleaning a camper interior and resetting an outdoor setup is less labor than a full cabin turnover.

Operating costs

Cabin operating costs are substantial. Expect to budget 30–45% of gross revenue for platform fees (Airbnb/VRBO typically take 3–5% from guests plus 3% from hosts, or 15–20% under host-only model), cleaning ($150–$300 per turnover), property management (20–30% of gross if using a manager), utilities, insurance, and maintenance reserves. Cabin maintenance costs — HVAC, roof, hot tub service, appliances — add up quickly.

ORG lot costs include platform fees, cleaning (typically $75–$150 per turnover for a camper), HOA dues (which cover resort amenities), lot-specific utilities, and insurance on the RV. The HOA cost is real, but it funds the amenities — pool, playground, putt-putt — that support occupancy and nightly rate.

Breakeven occupancy

This is the most useful number to calculate before you buy. Breakeven occupancy is the percentage of available nights you need to book just to cover all annual costs (debt service + operating expenses).

A $500K cabin with $33,600 annual debt service and $28,000 in operating costs needs to generate $61,600/year gross. At an average net rate of $220/night after platform fees, that's 280 nights — roughly 77% occupancy. If the market delivers 65%, you're losing money every year. If it delivers 80%, you're cash-flow positive but your margin is thin.

An ORG lot at a lower purchase price with lower debt service and lower operating costs per night has a structurally lower breakeven. Many well-positioned lots reach breakeven at 50–60% occupancy, which provides meaningful cushion against slow seasons, platform algorithm changes, or a year where personal use competes with rental weeks.

Where cabins win

Cabins have a higher gross revenue ceiling. A well-reviewed, well-located cabin with a mountain view, hot tub, and game room can generate $80K–$150K+ gross in a strong year. Top-performing cabins in Gatlinburg's vacation rental market are genuine income properties that outpace RV lots on absolute dollar return. They also appeal to a broader guest profile — couples, families, corporate retreats, bachelorette groups — giving them more demand diversification.

Cabin values have also appreciated meaningfully in the Smoky Mountain market over the past decade, though the entry price means you're making a bigger bet on that appreciation continuing.

Where RV lots win

Lower entry. Lower breakeven. Simpler operations. For buyers who already own an RV, the effective acquisition cost drops further — you're buying a lot, not two assets at once. The cash-flow risk profile is better for first-time STR investors or for investors who don't want to rely on hitting 75%+ occupancy just to stay even.

ORG lots also benefit from a self-contained resort environment: guests book knowing exactly what they're getting. The amenities, community, and consistent experience reduce the variance that comes with managing a standalone cabin where a bad review about a leaky roof can crater your occupancy for a season.

The honest summary

If your goal is maximum gross revenue and you have the capital, risk tolerance, and operational bandwidth to run a cabin rental in a competitive Smoky Mountain market, cabins can outperform. If your goal is a lower-risk, lower-complexity investment with a realistic path to cash-flow positive in year two or three, an ORG RV lot — especially with an existing camper — is often the cleaner bet. The numbers support the thesis. Run your own model before deciding either way.
