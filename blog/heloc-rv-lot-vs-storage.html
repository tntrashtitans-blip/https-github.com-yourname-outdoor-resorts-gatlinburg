How to Use a HELOC to Buy an RV Lot — and Why It Beats Paying Storage

Storage is a sunk cost. A HELOC builds equity. Here's the 10-year math that makes this comparison obvious once you run it.

A lot of RV owners are in the same position: they own a camper they love, it sits in a storage facility nine months a year, they're paying $200–$400 a month for the privilege, and they've been doing this for years. At some point someone floats the idea of buying a lot at a resort. The math usually ends the conversation quickly — in favor of the lot.

The storage cost most people don't actually calculate

RV storage costs in the Knoxville–Gatlinburg area vary significantly by facility type. Uncovered outdoor storage runs $100–$200/month. Covered storage runs $200–$350. Enclosed (climate-controlled or indoor) runs $350–$500+. Most owners land somewhere in the middle — a covered spot at a decent facility for around $250–$300/month.

At $275/month, here's the 10-year picture:

  • Year 1: $3,300
  • Year 5: $16,500
  • Year 10: $33,000

That's $33,000 out the door over a decade, with zero asset to show for it, no appreciation, no write-off, no personal use benefit beyond a parking spot. And it doesn't account for likely storage rate increases over that period.

10-year comparison: storage vs. HELOC-funded lot

The scenario below models a homeowner with enough equity to draw a HELOC for a lot purchase. Lot price and HELOC rate are illustrative — run the model with your actual numbers.

The HELOC payment is similar to what you were already spending on storage — except it's building toward something. Over 10 years, the gap between the two outcomes can easily exceed $100,000 when you include rental income and equity accumulation.

Why a HELOC specifically

Rate: HELOCs are typically priced at prime plus a margin — lower than RV purchase loans and often competitive with land loans, especially for borrowers with strong credit and substantial equity. Rates are variable, so they carry rate risk, but the starting cost of capital is usually favorable.

Speed: HELOCs can be established in 2–4 weeks with a lender you already have a relationship with. Having an open line before you identify a lot means you can move quickly when the right lot comes available — a real advantage in a market where well-positioned lots don't sit long.

Potential deductibility: Interest on a HELOC used to acquire, build, or substantially improve a qualified residence may be deductible. Whether an investment lot qualifies depends on your specific situation and tax filing. This is not tax advice — your CPA needs to make this call for your circumstances.

Flexibility: A HELOC is a revolving line. You draw what you need, pay it down as rental income comes in, and have access to the remaining line for future needs (improvements to the lot, personal expenses, other opportunities). This is structurally more flexible than a fixed amortization loan.

The personal use advantage people underestimate

When your camper is in storage, using it requires: calling ahead, driving to the facility, hitching or loading up, planning a destination, and then reversing the process on return. It's a meaningful friction that causes many RV owners to use their unit far less than they expected when they bought it.

When your camper lives at ORG on a lot you own, it's always set up. The awning is out, the chairs are there, the outdoor kitchen is ready. You show up and start your weekend. Your unit is never mid-season in a storage lot when the weather turns perfect. You are an owner at a Smoky Mountain resort, with resort amenities, a known community, and a standing invitation.

That quality-of-use difference is real. For many buyers, it's more than enough reason on its own — the investment math is the bonus.

⚠

HELOC rates are variable and can increase. If rates rise significantly, your payment increases. Model a stress scenario — what does your budget look like if your HELOC rate rises 2–3 percentage points? Rental income should provide a buffer, but know your worst-case before you draw.

Who this works for

This path works best for homeowners who have built meaningful equity (at least $80K–$150K available to draw), own an RV in good condition that can rent immediately without significant investment, are comfortable with variable-rate debt, and want to reduce the friction of personal RV use while building a tangible asset. If you're paying storage right now and have the equity, the math almost always favors making the move.
