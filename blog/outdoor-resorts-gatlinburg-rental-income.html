What Do ORG Lots Actually Rent For?

Realistic seasonal rates, the full fee picture, and a conservative year-one income model. What you should expect before you buy, not after.

There's no shortage of optimistic rental income projections in real estate marketing. This article tries to do the opposite: build a conservative, realistic picture of what an ORG lot can generate in year one, and what drives the gap between top performers and average units.

Seasonal rate ranges by lot position

Rates at ORG follow the Smoky Mountain tourism calendar closely. Summer (June–August) and fall foliage season (October) are peak. Spring (April–May) and the winter holiday stretch (Thanksgiving through New Year's) are strong shoulder. January through March is the slowest stretch — though the Smokies see less of a winter cliff than many other vacation markets.

Conservative year-one income model

Year one is almost always below stabilized performance. You're building reviews, refining your listing, and learning your seasonal demand pattern. The model below assumes a mid-tier interior-to-lake-area lot with a quality but not premium camper unit.

Assumes 65% occupancy, 150 nights rented, average net rate $125/night

By year two, with established reviews and better seasonal pricing strategy, gross typically climbs to $26K–$32K, and NOI rises accordingly. Top-performing creekside lots with premium camper setups have reported gross revenue of $35K–$45K in strong years — but those are outliers with specific location and presentation advantages.

The conservative year-one range across the ORG market is approximately $18,000–$28,000 gross, with $7,000–$14,000 NOI before debt service. Where you land in that range depends more on lot position and listing quality than on any single factor you can't control.

What separates top performers from average units

Lot location: Creekside lots outperform on both rate and occupancy. The sound of running water is a legitimate differentiator in a market with competing inventory. Lake-area lots have strong demand. Interior lots compete primarily on price and presentation quality.

Photos: Professional photography — or even a clean smartphone shoot with good natural light — has a measurable impact on booking conversion. A listing with twelve high-quality photos outperforms one with four mediocre photos at the same price point, consistently.

Setup and outdoor space: Guests at ORG are paying for an outdoor experience. A covered outdoor area, quality chairs, a functional grill, and string lights convert browsers into bookers. These are low-cost improvements with high visibility in listing photos.

Review velocity: Platform algorithms reward new listings with visibility — temporarily. That initial boost matters. Coming in with a clean, well-staged unit and a responsive host profile helps you capture that early window and build the review count that sustains ranking afterward.

Pricing strategy: Using dynamic pricing tools (Wheelhouse, PriceLabs, Beyond) rather than flat rates can improve annual revenue by 10–18% by capturing demand spikes at events, holidays, and peak weekends without leaving money on the table during slower stretches.

Location ladder at ORG

In order of rental income potential: creekside > lake-adjacent > near amenities (pool, playground) > interior. This order holds consistently. When evaluating a specific lot, ask for the lot's rental history and cross-reference against its map position. A lot that underperforms relative to its position usually has a presentation or management explanation, not a structural one.
