What to Know About 1031 Exchanges Into RV Resort Property

How the exchange works, why lots qualify, the timeline you cannot miss, and what to get right on depreciation and investment intent.

⚠

This article is informational only. It does not constitute tax, legal, or financial advice. 1031 exchanges involve strict IRS rules, qualified intermediary requirements, and significant financial consequences if mishandled. Work with a licensed CPA, a qualified intermediary (QI), and a real estate attorney on any actual exchange.

What a 1031 exchange is

Under Section 1031 of the Internal Revenue Code, when you sell an investment property, you can defer federal capital gains taxes by reinvesting the proceeds into a "like-kind" replacement property. The tax isn't eliminated — it's deferred until you eventually sell the replacement property without doing another 1031. Some investors roll exchanges indefinitely and defer the tax liability for decades.

The basic requirements:

  • Both the relinquished property and the replacement property must be held for investment or productive use in a trade or business
  • The properties must be "like-kind" — for real property, this is broadly defined; most investment real estate is like-kind to other investment real estate
  • A qualified intermediary (QI) must hold the proceeds between the sale and the purchase — you cannot touch the money
  • The 45-day and 180-day deadlines are absolute; there are no extensions except in narrow, federally declared disaster situations

Why RV lots generally qualify

An RV lot held for investment or rental purposes is real property under IRC §1031. It is like-kind to other investment real property — rental houses, commercial buildings, raw land, or any other real estate you were holding for investment. The lot doesn't need to match the type of property you're selling; it just needs to be real property held for investment.

The critical qualifier is "held for investment." If you buy an ORG lot and immediately use it exclusively for personal purposes, the IRS will challenge investment intent. If you establish a rental program, document your rental activity, and limit personal use — particularly in the first year or two — the investment character of the property is supported.

Lots with existing campers already in a rental program are ideal 1031 replacement properties: they can be identified and closed quickly, they have documented rental history, and they arrive with investment-use evidence already in place.

The timeline you cannot miss

Before Day 0 Engage QI
Engage your qualified intermediary before the relinquished property closes

The QI must be in place before closing. If proceeds hit your account — even briefly — the exchange is disqualified. Choose your QI early and confirm the closing attorney knows to wire directly to the QI.

Day 0 Relinquished property closes
Both clocks start simultaneously

The 45-day identification clock and the 180-day exchange clock both start on the closing date of the relinquished property. They run concurrently — Day 180 does not begin after Day 45.

Days 1–45 Identification period
Identify up to three replacement properties in writing to your QI

You must identify properties in writing by Day 45 — no extensions. You can identify up to three properties of any value (the "three-property rule") or more properties under the 200% rule. Have your shortlist of ORG lots prepared before your relinquished property closes.

Days 46–180 Exchange period
Close on one or more identified replacement properties

The QI transfers exchange funds directly to the replacement property closing. You cannot close on a property not on your identification list. If you need financing, get pre-approved before Day 45 — lenders take time.

Post-close Document and maintain
Document investment intent immediately and consistently

Establish a rental program, execute a property management or listing agreement, and file the appropriate IRS forms with your tax return. Your CPA will report the exchange on Form 8824.

Depreciation: what you can and cannot deduct

Land cannot be depreciated. The lot itself — the underlying real property — has no depreciable basis under IRS rules. This is true regardless of how much you paid for the lot.

The RV or camper on the lot may be depreciable. If the camper is personal property (not real property) and is used in a rental activity, it may be depreciable as a business asset — typically over a 5-year schedule as listed property under MACRS. Cost segregation analysis can identify and separate the depreciable components from the land.

When a lot with an existing camper is acquired in a 1031 exchange, the allocation between land value and personal property value affects your depreciation schedule. Your CPA should establish a clear allocation at acquisition to maximize allowable deductions.

Investment intent: what the IRS looks for

The IRS does not have a bright-line rule for how much personal use disqualifies a 1031 replacement property. But it looks at the facts and circumstances of your use. Factors that support investment intent:

  • Establishing a short-term rental program within a reasonable time after closing
  • Using a listing platform or property manager with documented bookings
  • Limiting personal use — particularly in year one — to a minor fraction of total rental days
  • Maintaining records of rental income, expenses, and bookings
  • Treating the property as a business asset on your tax returns from day one

Factors that undermine investment intent: taking personal vacations at the property before establishing a rental program, converting it to primary personal use shortly after acquisition, or failing to ever rent it at all after acquiring it through an exchange.

Who should consider this path

1031 exchange buyers at ORG are typically investors who've sold a rental house, a piece of raw land, or a commercial property and want to deploy gains into a lower-complexity investment with personal use potential. The Smoky Mountains tourism market provides the demand foundation; the lot's position within a managed resort reduces operational friction; and the 1031 structure eliminates the immediate tax hit that would otherwise reduce the capital available to invest.

If you're sitting on significant unrealized gains in an investment property you're ready to sell, and you've never modeled the 1031 path into ORG, it's worth a conversation with your CPA before you list.
