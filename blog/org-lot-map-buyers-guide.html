Know the Layout Before You Buy: An ORG Lot Map Walkthrough for Investors

The campground map is the most useful tool in your due diligence kit. Here's how to read it as an investor.

The ORG lot map. Use this as a reference tool when evaluating any specific lot listing — position within the resort is one of the strongest predictors of rental income.

Before you tour a specific lot or make an offer, spend time with the campground map. Lot position at ORG is one of the strongest predictors of rental income potential, nightly rate ceiling, and long-term value. A buyer who understands the layout makes better decisions than one who's evaluating lots in isolation without the spatial context.

The four lot types and what they mean for investors

Tier 1 — Highest demand

Creekside lots

The most sought-after positions at ORG. The sound of running water is a genuine guest amenity — it shows in listing photos, gets mentioned in reviews, and supports both higher nightly rates and higher occupancy. Creekside lots command the highest purchase prices and the strongest rental income. If your budget allows, this is where you want to be.

Tier 2 — Strong demand

Lake-area lots

Water views and proximity to the fishing pond are strong guest draws. Lake-area lots don't quite match creekside rates but outperform interior lots consistently. They also photograph well — a listing with water visible in background shots books better than one without. Solid investment positions with good liquidity if you ever want to sell.

Tier 3 — Good value

Interior lots

Lower entry price, lower nightly rate ceiling, but still solid rental demand within the ORG resort environment. Interior lots with good shade, mature trees, or proximity to amenities outperform their peers. For buyers focused on breakeven and cash flow rather than rate maximization, interior lots often present the most accessible path to investment-positive ownership.

Tier 4 — Amenity-adjacent

Near resort amenities

Lots near the pool, playground, game room, or putt-putt course have positioning advantages for family-focused guests. These don't command the premium of creekside lots, but they perform above comparable interior lots that lack amenity proximity. Look for these when creekside is out of budget and you want a defensible rental position.

How to use the map before making an offer

When a specific lot comes to your attention, pull up the map and orient the lot number within its section. Ask these questions:

  • Is it genuinely creekside, or just near the creek? There's a meaningful rate difference between a lot with audible creek access and one that's two rows back.
  • Which direction does the lot face? Morning shade is preferable in Tennessee summers — a lot that bakes in afternoon sun will get complaints about heat in July and August.
  • What's on the neighboring lots? A lot between two well-maintained, rented units is a better investment than one flanked by long-term owner units that sit empty for months.
  • How far is it from the entrance? Easy access from the resort road reduces guest navigation friction. First-time visitors who can't find their lot leave negative reviews.
  • Is it on a corner or an end position? Corner lots often have more green space and feel less crowded — this matters to guests and shows in photos.

What to look for beyond location

Paving and pad condition: A well-paved, level pad is easier to photograph, easier for guests to set up on, and signals a well-maintained lot. Check for cracking, settling, or drainage issues that would require remediation.

Shade and tree cover: Mature trees are a genuine amenity. They keep units cooler in summer (lowering utility costs and guest comfort complaints), create a sense of privacy, and photograph beautifully. A heavily shaded creekside lot outperforms an exposed creekside lot in both listing appeal and operating cost.

Parking: Two vehicles need to park comfortably. Tight lots with no guest parking overhang into the road create friction. Confirm the pad accommodates the unit plus two vehicles without obstructing neighbors or resort traffic.

Neighbor lot condition: A lot next to a derelict or neglected unit will underperform. Your listing photos will capture the neighbor's setup whether you want them to or not. If a neighboring lot is poorly maintained, factor in the visual drag on your listing before offering at a premium price.

The map is a reference tool, not a substitute for a physical visit. Walk the lot and its immediate neighbors before closing. Satellite imagery and lot maps won't show you the shade pattern at 2pm or the sound the creek actually makes from that spot.

Building your shortlist from the map

Use the map to identify three to five positions that represent your target criteria before you start making calls or scheduling tours. This keeps your evaluation disciplined and prevents you from falling in love with a lot before you've compared it to alternatives. When a well-positioned lot comes available, you'll be ready to move — 1031 exchange buyers especially need this preparation in place before Day 0.

Map-based due diligence checklist

  • Identify lot tier: creekside / lake-area / interior / amenity-adjacent
  • Note lot orientation and estimated sun exposure
  • Check proximity to resort entrance and road access
  • Identify neighboring lot owner types (rental vs. personal use)
  • Flag corner or end positions for priority consideration
  • Cross-reference map position against available rental history or comparable rental data
  • Schedule a physical visit to walk the lot before making any offer
