Ownership & Investing

Outdoor Resorts Gatlinburg lots for sale — an investor's guide

Most buyers come in through one of three doors: they finance the lot and a new RV, they place a camper they already own, or they roll gains from a previous sale tax-deferred. Each path has a different math and a different risk profile.

Scenario 01

Buying a lot and financing an RV

This is the highest-cost entry path. Two loans, two debt service obligations — but rental income can change the math more than most buyers expect before they run the numbers.

The lot financing challenge

RV lots at ORG don't always qualify for conventional 30-year mortgages. Expect land loans or portfolio lenders at 10–20% down, often with shorter amortization (15–20 years). Rate and term vary. Get pre-approved before you identify a lot.

RV loan pressure

RV loans are personal property loans — typically 10–20 years, rates ranging 7–11% depending on year and credit. A $60K RV at 9% over 15 years is roughly $610/month. That number needs to live comfortably inside your rental revenue forecast.

How rental income changes the math

A well-positioned lot with a quality unit can generate $18K–$28K gross annually. At $22K gross with a 35% expense ratio, you're netting ~$14,300 — enough to cover most RV loan payments and contribute toward lot debt service.

What to run before you offer

Model three scenarios: conservative (slow ramp, 55% occupancy), base (75% occupancy by year two), and optimistic (top-performer, creekside location). Know your breakeven occupancy rate. If the unit needs to hit 80% to be cash-flow positive, that's a risk flag.

HOA fees as a marketing asset

ORG's HOA dues cover amenities guests actually pay for: pool, putt-putt, playground, event calendar. Don't treat HOA as pure overhead — it supports the nightly rate and occupancy you're underwriting against.

Two loans means two exit options

If you later want to reduce exposure, you can sell the RV and continue renting the lot to others, or sell the lot-and-unit together as a turnkey investment. Keeping the assets separable is a structural advantage.

Considerations before you proceed

  • Get pre-approved for a land/lot loan before making offers — not all lenders serve this asset class
  • Budget 8–12 months to reach stabilized occupancy; year one will underperform year two
  • Confirm HOA dues, lot-specific assessments, and any utility pass-throughs in the seller's disclosures
  • Ask for two to three years of rental history if the lot is an existing rental; verify against platform statements
  • Determine whether the RV qualifies as a second home for any tax purposes — consult your CPA

Scenario 02

Buying a lot and placing your existing camper

If you already own a travel trailer, fifth wheel, or motorhome and it's sitting in storage or in a driveway, this is frequently the most financially efficient path to ORG ownership.

Storage is a sunk cost

RV storage in the Knoxville–Gatlinburg corridor runs $150–$500/month depending on covered vs. uncovered and facility quality. Over 10 years that's $18K–$60K with nothing to show for it. A lot purchase converts that monthly drain into equity-building debt service.

The HELOC comparison

If you have equity in a primary residence, a HELOC at current rates can fund the lot purchase with potentially deductible interest. The payment structure often mirrors what you were already spending on storage — except it builds toward an asset you own outright.

Running the real numbers

Lot prices at ORG vary by position. Creekside lots command a premium; interior lots offer better entry pricing and solid rental demand. Budget for closing costs, any HOA initiation fee, and whatever condition work the unit needs before its first rental.

Why this has the best risk profile

You arrive with one loan (the lot), not two. The RV is already paid for. Your cash-flow breakeven is lower, your debt service is simpler, and your personal use is fully available without displacing rental inventory you financed.

What makes a good rental unit

Guests book presentation. A clean, well-maintained unit with updated bedding, working AC, a functional outdoor kitchen or grill setup, and good photos outperforms a newer unit that photos poorly. Platform ratings compound — your first season sets your trajectory.

Personal use and IRS rules

If you rent for more than 14 days and use personally for more than 14 days or 10% of rental days, the IRS treats the property as a personal residence — which limits expense deductions. If investment intent is primary, document accordingly and consult your CPA.

10-year math snapshot

  • Storage cost (10 yrs @ $300/mo): $36,000 spent, no asset
  • Lot loan (10 yrs, principal paydown): equity built, rental income partially offsets payments
  • Net position difference at year 10: you own a Smoky Mountain lot vs. a receipt from a storage facility
  • This comparison sharpens further if rental income covers 50–80% of annual debt service
  • Lot appreciation in resort communities has historically trended positive — though past performance is not a guarantee

Scenario 03

Using a 1031 exchange to invest at ORG

Investors who've sold rental properties, raw land, or other investment real estate can potentially defer capital gains taxes by rolling proceeds into an ORG lot — provided the exchange is structured correctly.

What a 1031 makes possible

Under IRC §1031, you can defer federal capital gains tax on the sale of investment property by identifying a like-kind replacement within 45 days and closing within 180 days. An RV lot held for investment or rental purposes generally qualifies as like-kind real property.

Lots with existing campers suit 1031 buyers

A lot with a camper already in place is easier to identify quickly (a 1031 requirement) and can demonstrate rental income history — both useful when your QI needs to document investment intent and your lender needs to underwrite the asset.

Depreciation: land vs. camper

Land cannot be depreciated. The RV or camper on the lot, however, is personal property and may be depreciable as a business asset if the unit is held for rental. Your CPA can model cost segregation and depreciation schedules specific to your situation.

Investment intent must be documented

Personal use after acquisition can jeopardize investment property status. If you intend to also use the lot personally, document a clear rental-first policy, use a property management agreement, and limit personal use during the first one to two years of ownership.

⚠

This page is informational only and does not constitute tax, legal, or financial advice. 1031 exchanges involve strict IRS timelines, qualified intermediary requirements, and significant tax consequences if mishandled. Consult a licensed CPA, qualified intermediary, and real estate attorney before proceeding.

1031 exchange timeline at a glance

1
Before you close the relinquished property Engage a qualified intermediary (QI). You cannot touch the proceeds — the QI must hold them.
2
Day 0 — Relinquished property closes The 45-day and 180-day clocks start simultaneously on the closing date.
3
Day 1–45 — Identification period Identify up to three replacement properties in writing to your QI. ORG lots with existing campers can be identified quickly — having a shortlist before Day 0 is strongly recommended.
4
Day 46–180 — Exchange period Close on one or more of your identified replacement properties. The QI transfers proceeds directly to the closing — you never receive the funds.
5
Post-close — Document investment intent Establish a rental program, limit personal use, and maintain records. Your CPA will need this documentation at tax time.
